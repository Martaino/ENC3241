# Proposal

## Assessing the Impact of Git Integration in Early Undergraduate Computer Science Curriculum
Martin Fanjul Guzman &emsp;&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;&emsp;&emsp; Computer Science BS

### Background
&emsp;&emsp;The computer science (CS) curriculum at the University of Central Florida (UCF) provides a good understanding of data structures, algorithms, and software development. However, it lacks instruction on version control systems (VCS) like Git. The ability to use VCS properly is becoming increasingly important in the software industry. When in teams, version control is one of the most important factors for communicating and sharing with other developers. (Haaranen and Lehtinen 87) In early courses at UCF, there is no integration of version control and files are shared through manual file management and .zip files. This causes difficulty in collaborative environments like team projects and could lead to difficulties using version control when it’s needed in the future. This proposal attempts to quantify the version control literacy or “Git-literacy” of UCF CS students through a study that will provide a framework for curriculum integration.

### Methodology
This research is focused on collecting data from a student-wide survey of CS students:
1.	Survey Design: I will use Qualtrics (provided by UCF) to conduct a 20-question survey. These questions will measure a student's current knowledge of Git commands, backing up code, and how well they can use version control in team projects. It will also ask them their confidence in using Git and if they would have benefited from learning Git early in the CS curriculum (if applicable).
2.	Recruitment: I will print 20 color flyers that will be posted around the UCF campus. Specifically, I will be posting these flyers around areas with more CS students like the Harris Engineering Center and the UCF Engineering II building. If that is not enough, I will also share the link of the survey digitally through UCF CS discord servers.
3.	Incentives: To make sure that people want to do the survey, I will be offering incentives. Students who complete the survey will be entered into a randomized drawing that will select 50 winners. Each winner will be sent a $10 Amazon e-gift card to their UCF e-mail address once the data collection has been completed.
4.	Data Analysis and Report: Once the 100 survey responses have been gathered, I will export the data into Excel and find correlations between the given data. The expected correlations would be a student’s current course level and their VCS proficiency, a student's VCS proficiency and their confidence, and a student’s current course level and if they would have appreciated version control in their curriculum.

### Anticipated Outcomes 
&emsp;&emsp;This research will result in a final report on the state of VCS knowledge in the UCF CS student body. I will also recommend curriculum changes for the CS department based on the dataset collected. I will present the report findings at the Showcase of Undergraduate Research Excellence (SURE) and the UCF Student Research Showcase in Spring 2027.

### Significance 
-	This proposal addresses version control, a crucial skill in the software industry. By learning Git in the computer science curriculum at UCF, students can increase their competitiveness in the job market. The research shows the importance of teaching Git and replacing the current method of learning Git, self-teaching.  
-	The people who will benefit from this work are undergraduate students in computer science and the UCF Department of Computer Science. This research can also be used to serve as a model for other curriculums in need of change.  
-	This research provides insight into how beneficial early exposure to version control affects student performance in the CS department. It will also highlight the unnecessary hardship caused by the lack of Git in the UCF Computer Science curriculum.
